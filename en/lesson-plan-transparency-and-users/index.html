Lesson plan: transparency and users

A debate-centred lesson on the user's right to know: when disclosure is owed, what it fixes, and — the productive part — what it does not fix.

Open the game Transparency page

01Audience, duration, objectives

  • Target audience — secondary (14+); works well with younger groups than the other plans
  • Duration — 50–60 minutes
  • Objectives — students can say when AI interaction must be disclosed, argue both the value and the limits of disclosure, and connect transparency to their own digital habits
  • Preparation — review transparency obligations; queue the game's chatbot case

02Lesson flow

  1. Provocation (10') — "You chatted with your city's helpdesk for 20 minutes. It was a bot. Does it matter?" Quick poll, three reasons per side on the board.
  2. The rule (10') — when the AI Act requires disclosure (chatbots, synthetic content); why the remedy here is honesty rather than heavy engineering.
  3. Debate (20') — motion: "A clear 'this is AI' label solves the problem." Two teams, structured turns; the teacher's role is to inject the hard cases (vulnerable users, viral deepfakes, reliance).
  4. Game verification (10') — play "The desk that always answers" together: does the class's debate position survive the case's evidence?

03Assessment and reflection

One-paragraph position: "A label is enough when … and not enough when …". Look for answers that name reliance or vulnerability — that is the lesson landing.

04Extension

Run the transparency hunt from the activities page as homework, reporting findings next lesson. Related background: digital citizenship and AI regulation.

Educational simulation — not legal advice

NO AI ACT is an independent, open-source educational tool. It deliberately simplifies Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act) and does not replace the legal text or professional advice.
