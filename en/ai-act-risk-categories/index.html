Risk-based approach · Core concept

The AI Act's risk categories

One idea organises the whole regulation: match the strictness of the rules to the risk of the use. This page walks through the levels and shows how each one appears in the game's cases.

Classify a case yourself Full guide

01Why risk, and not technology

A face-recognition library can unlock your phone or scan a public square; a scoring model can rank loan offers or rank citizens. Regulating "the algorithm" would either ban too much or too little. The Act therefore regulates uses in context: purpose, data, affected people, consequences.

For learners this is the single most transferable idea — and the reason the game asks you to read a dossier before classifying anything.

02The four levels at a glance

Banned

Unacceptable risk

Practices considered incompatible with fundamental rights — e.g. generalized social scoring. Prohibited outright.

Strict duties

High risk

Allowed, but with risk management, quality data, documentation and effective human oversight.

Must inform

Transparency

Situations where people must know AI is involved: chatbots, synthetic content, deepfakes.

Everyday uses

Minimal risk

Most applications — spam filters, game AI, recommendations — face no specific new obligations.

03Unacceptable risk: the red lines

The prohibited practices are few and specific. What makes them teachable is their logic: harms so structural that no amount of oversight fixes them. The detailed page — with the classic boundary cases — is prohibited AI practices.

04High risk: allowed, but earned

Education, employment, essential services, health: here AI can help or quietly decide people's lives. The Act's answer is obligations, not bans — explored on high-risk AI systems.

05Transparency situations

Sometimes the risk is simply not knowing: mistaking a bot for a person, a synthetic image for a photo. The remedy is disclosure — see transparency obligations.

06Minimal risk — and why it matters didactically

Most AI is fine. Teaching this prevents the two classic failure modes: "everything is banned" alarmism and "nothing changes" complacency. A good exercise is sorting mundane examples (spellcheck, playlist ranking) to establish the baseline before the hard cases.

07The categories in the game

The game's 11 cases deliberately span the ladder: clear prohibitions (the city of scores), high-risk systems (triage, recruitment, adaptive EdTech), transparency cases (the synthetic city, the always-answering desk) and the mirror cases where the same mechanism lands differently by context. The risk-based lesson plan builds a full session on exactly this.

Educational simulation — not legal advice

NO AI ACT is an independent, open-source educational tool. It deliberately simplifies Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act) and does not replace the legal text or professional advice.
