## VIDEO 2: "Why AI Needs Rules (Real-World Examples)"
**Target:** Mid-level, policy-interested
**Duration:** 4:30
**Keywords:** AI bias, AI ethics, AI regulation, AI discrimination

### SCRIPT

**[0:00-0:20] HOOK**
"An AI system just denied someone a mortgage. A hiring algorithm rejected 100 qualified candidates. A facial recognition system sent an innocent person to jail. Are these bugs or features? Let's talk about why AI needs rules."

**[0:20-1:00] EXAMPLE 1: HIRING**
"Amazon built an AI hiring system. Sounds efficient, right? But the algorithm trained on 10 years of hiring data — which was mostly male engineers.

Result: The AI learned to discriminate against women. It actively downranked female candidates' resumes.

Amazon scrapped the project. But thousands of companies never caught theirs."

**[1:00-1:45] EXAMPLE 2: CREDIT SCORING**
"A financial institution used an AI to decide loan approvals. On paper, it was neutral — just looking at income, credit history, employment.

But the algorithm learned to use zip code as a proxy for race. People in historically redlined neighborhoods got denied even with good credit.

This is called 'disparate impact.' It's discrimination, even if it's unintentional."

**[1:45-2:30] EXAMPLE 3: HEALTHCARE**
"A hospital deployed an AI to predict who needs intensive care. Seems logical — let the algorithm spot patterns humans miss.

But the algorithm trained on data reflecting existing inequities. It systematized bias:
- Underestimating risk for Black patients
- Overestimating risk for white patients

Result: Thousands of people got wrong priority levels."

**[2:30-3:15] THE PATTERN**
"These aren't one-off failures. They're systemic:

1. **Bias in training data** — AI learns from history, which includes human bias
2. **Opaque decision-making** — You can't see why you were rejected
3. **Scale** — One buggy algorithm affects millions instantly
4. **Accountability gap** — Who's responsible when an AI makes a mistake?

The EU AI Act addresses each of these."

**[3:15-4:15] HOW THE AI ACT HELPS**
"The law requires companies to:
- Document where training data comes from (no hidden bias)
- Test for discrimination before deployment
- Keep humans in the loop for high-stakes decisions
- Be transparent with users ('this decision was made by AI')
- Take legal responsibility if something goes wrong

It doesn't ban AI. It says: if you use AI to make decisions about people, you're accountable."

## VIDEO 4: "How to Audit Your AI System (Checklist)"
**Target:** Technical/compliance teams
**Duration:** 3:30
**Keywords:** AI audit, AI compliance checklist, EU AI Act compliance

### SCRIPT

**[0:00-0:15] HOOK**
"You have 6 months to audit your AI systems for EU AI Act compliance. Here's the checklist we use."

**[0:15-1:00] STEP 1: CLASSIFY YOUR AI**
"First, identify which category your AI falls into:

Ask:
- Could it significantly impact fundamental rights? (Privacy, free expression, non-discrimination)
- Does it affect critical infrastructure, employment, healthcare, or biometrics?
- Could it cause physical or economic harm?

If you answer yes to any, it's likely high-risk. High-risk AI must be audited and registered.

Document this in a spreadsheet. You'll need it."

**[1:00-1:45] STEP 2: DATA AUDIT**
"High-risk AI must have documented training data:

Check:
- Where does your data come from? (User data, public datasets, third-party sources?)
- Is data representative? (Does it cover all demographics?)
- Are there known biases in the data? (Historical inequities, labeling errors?)
- Can you reproduce the training set? (Regulatory verification may require this)
- Do you have licenses for all data?

Document your findings. This is not optional."

**[1:45-2:30] STEP 3: BIAS & FAIRNESS TESTING**
"Test your model for discrimination:

Run:
- Performance parity analysis (does accuracy vary by demographic group?)
- Equalized odds testing (same true positive rates across groups?)
- Demographic parity checks (similar approval rates across groups?)
- Intersectionality testing (combined effects of multiple protected characteristics?)

If your system shows bias, you must either:
- Retrain with better data
- Adjust decision thresholds to correct for bias
- Add human review for borderline cases
- Document the bias and justify why you can't fix it

'We didn't know' is not a legal defense."

**[2:30-3:15] STEP 4: IMPLEMENTATION AUDIT**
"Once deployed, verify:

- Is a qualified human actually reviewing decisions? (Not just a checkbox)
- Can users access explanations? (Why were they denied?)
- Is the system logged and audited? (Can you prove what happened?)
- Are you monitoring real-world performance? (Bias can emerge in production)
- What's your incident response? (If something goes wrong, what's the plan?)

This is ongoing. Compliance doesn't end at launch."

## VIDEO 5: "AI Act Myths: What It Actually Does (and Doesn't)"
**Target:** General + Decision-makers
**Duration:** 3:00
**Keywords:** AI Act myths, AI regulation misconceptions

### SCRIPT

**[0:00-0:15] HOOK**
"There's a lot of confusion about the EU AI Act. Let's bust some myths."

**[0:15-0:50] MYTH 1: "It Bans AI"**
"False. The AI Act is strict, but it doesn't ban artificial intelligence.

It bans specific high-harm uses:
- Social credit scoring (rating people as citizens)
- Certain biometric surveillance (mass facial recognition)
- Subliminal manipulation (dark patterns designed to exploit psychology)

Everything else? Allowed, but with oversight appropriate to the risk level.

ChatGPT, recommendation algorithms, most hiring tools — all still legal."

**[0:50-1:20] MYTH 2: "Only Big Tech is Affected"**
"False. The law applies to:
- Any company deploying AI in the EU
- Any company selling to EU customers
- That includes startups, consultancies, and smaller firms

If you're a mid-market company using an AI system for hiring or lending, you need to comply — regardless of size."

**[1:20-1:50] MYTH 3: "Compliance is Optional"**
"False. The AI Act is enforceable law. Non-compliance triggers:
- Fines up to €20 million or 4% of global revenue
- Lawsuits from affected individuals
- System shutdown orders from regulators

This isn't guidance. It's regulatory obligation with enforcement teeth."

**[1:50-2:20] MYTH 4: "It's Too Strict to Innovate"**
"Debatable. The Act requires transparency and testing, which slows time-to-market for high-risk AI. But it doesn't ban innovation — it channels it toward responsible deployment.

Think of it like pharmaceutical regulation: Drugs undergo testing before market. This prevents harm but doesn't stop drug development.

The tradeoff: slower, safer AI deployment."

**[2:20-2:50] MYTH 5: "It Only Applies to Europe"**
"Partially true. The law applies to:
- AI systems deployed in the EU
- Companies selling to EU customers
- Any global company meeting those criteria

But it creates a ripple effect. Many companies adopt EU standards globally rather than maintaining two versions. So even non-EU companies often comply."
